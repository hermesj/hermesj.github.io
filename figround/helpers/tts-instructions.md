The Siren text lives in an entirely different register — where the Odysseus text is confined and inward, this one is expansive, painterly, almost liturgical.
1) Scene direction
Before dawn, outdoors, somewhere high above the sea — a clifftop, a terrace, the roof of a coastal house. The reader is alone, facing east, waiting for light that has not yet arrived. She is not telling a story. She is performing a ritual she has performed many times before, one that sustains something — the world, perhaps, or only herself. There is no audience, and that is the point. The voice carries out over open water, not to be heard, but because it must.
2) Sample reading context
The opening paragraphs are slow, luminous, unhurried — the voice of someone painting a landscape with great care. Long, flowing sentences should be given room to breathe; let the rhythm of the prose do the work. Think of a camera moving steadily through space — the dawn, the planets, the kingfishers. There is wonder here but no urgency. The tone is one of ancient familiarity: this morning has happened before, many times.
The kingfisher passage accelerates. The syntax itself picks up speed — "gaining colour and form at breathtaking speed" — and the reading should follow, becoming lighter, more airborne, without losing control. Through the cliffs, up the stone pillars, the voice should feel like it is climbing.
When the Siren appears, everything stills again. "A delicate being appears" — this needs space around it, silence before and after. The final sentence of the paragraph — "She sings a faery song" — should arrive simply, almost as a fact, without drama. What follows will supply all the drama needed.
The song passage is the heart of the text, and it must not be rushed. It builds in waves, not in steps — each image swelling into the next. The voice should feel sustained, carried, as though the reader herself is being moved by what she is reading. The rhetorical questions — "What force might it unleash where longings lie in chains" — should open outward rather than close downward, reaching rather than insisting. The final line — "As long as she sings, the Siren is the most beautiful of all beings" — is not a climax but a condition. It holds within it the knowledge that the singing will end.
After the song, the tone deepens. The paragraph about poets, dancers, painters — this is where the Siren's loneliness becomes universal. Read it with tenderness but without sentimentality. The list of unwitnessed artists is not a lament; it is a statement of what is. "Not until evening does one solitary tear escape her" — one tear, no more, and the voice should match that restraint.
The final paragraph is a slow fade to black. Everything mechanical, drained of life — the ship motionless, the kingfishers pulled back as though on strings. "The world is silence" — three words that mirror the Odysseus text's "The chains hold." Deliver them the same way: quiet, final, without emphasis. Not a pair, not a list. Two things that define each other by what they are not. "Figure" lands first, steady, present. A brief pause on "and" — not dramatic, just enough to let the space between them exist. Then "Ground," slightly lower in pitch, as though it were always already there, waiting to be noticed.
The subtitles belong to their respective texts and should take on their character. "Odysseus. The Siren. An Attempt." — three full stops, three separate statements. Each one placed deliberately, with the quiet gravity of someone naming something they are not entirely sure they should have named. "An Attempt" carries a faint self-deprecation — this is not a boast, it is an admission.
"The Song of the Siren" — no stops, one flowing phrase. It should sound like the beginning of something, an opening gesture, the way a title page feels before you turn it. Warmer, more certain of itself than the Odysseus subtitle, but also more solitary.
